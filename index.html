Art and Identity in the Digital Age


Long before we learned to write code or text, our ancestors were already using art as their "first language." But for me, connecting to this visual language was not easy. I envied classmates who were naturally good at art.

I remember my MSEP classes well: I aced written exams but struggled with paper mache and could only draw stickmen. I realized that art is not just about being good at holding a paintbrush—it is about the desire to express a message.

As a future IT professional, I learned a programmer is like a digital painter, and a developer is like a sculptor building a functional reality from raw code. I felt this "artistic satisfaction" when I started programming.

The feeling of fixing a bug or making the code work perfectly is just like the joy a painter has when they finish their masterpiece. Coding became my way of carving logic to make it real.

Technology is the modern "canvas," and digital tools are our chisels. As a Gen Z and digital native, I want to use these tools to show who we Filipinos really are.

I want to design platforms that reflect unique Filipino values: specifically "Bayanihan" and our genuine warmth. In a digital world that is often cold, my goal is to fill our interfaces with hospitality, proving that technology, like art, can also have a soul.

Kundiman by Rob Daniel

DIGITAL HARANA

This artwork reimagines the traditional Filipino 'harana' as a metaphor for digital prudence, where the guitar strings strum glowing streams of positive data to push back the chaos of online misinformation and toxicity.

It illustrates the deliberate choice to project meaningful, truthful messages amidst the noise of 'error codes' and digital static, symbolizing the modern Filipino value of delicadeza.

Ultimately, it serves as a reminder that in a noisy digital world, our greatest influence lies in the integrity and quality of the content we choose to share.

ARTWORK ANALYSIS

Benedicto Cabrera (b. 1942)

Woman with Pillow

Signed and dated 1981 (lower right)

Mixed media

12 1/2" x 10" (31 cm x 25 cm)

Pormalistiko

Formalist Analysis

At first glance, the artwork shows a woman sitting on the ground, hugging a pillow tightly while covering part of her face. The composition is "centered" or placed in the middle, which emphasizes the extreme isolation of the character.

BenCab uses his signature "Sabel" style: this is seen in the lines that seem to move and flow in her dress, which opposes the hard and steady form of the pillow. The colors are pale and seem dirty (muted tones of brown, gray, and off-white), which suggests poverty, sadness, and a connection to the ground. The contrast between the dark background and the yellow pillow pulls the viewer's eyes toward the pillow, which is the center of attention and her only source of comfort.

Istruktural

Structural Approach

The Pillow

In a general view, a pillow is a symbol of rest or sleep. But here, it serves as a "shield." It is a barrier between the woman and the harsh world. It can also be a symbol of her dreams that she tries hard to embrace, or perhaps it is the only soft thing in her rough life.

The Bare Feet

This is a direct symbol of poverty and being "vulnerable." It shows that she belongs to the masses, to the people who walk barefoot and feel the hardness of the ground.

The Act of Clinging

The structure of her body which is curved inward suggests self-protection. She buries herself in the pillow because it is the only thing that gives her security.

The Woman

The figure represents resilience in fragility. She exists, she feels, she endures.

Sosyolohikal

Social Realism & Context

1. Historical Context

Although Martial Law was "lifted" or removed under Proclamation 2045 during this year, the power and oppression of the regime remained. It was a time of pretending to have freedom, but economic suffering and lack of justice continued.

2. The Representation of "Sabel"

The woman in the picture is based on BenCab's "Sabel" series: a real scavenger he saw in Tondo. She is the face of the "disenfranchised" or the Filipinos who were left behind and became victims of society.

3. Critical Message

Woman with Pillow is a criticism of society. It shows that despite the political changes in 1981, the common Filipino remained miserable. The only "comfort" the poor could get was sleep or escaping reality using a pillow. This reflects a "social cancer" where those at the bottom have nowhere to run but to themselves.

Sikolohikal

“Quiet Desperation”

It does not scream in anger, but rather whispers extreme exhaustion. Her act of covering her face suggests shame or a desire to hide. She does not want the world to see her, and she also does not want to see the world.

I also feel her anxiety as her position is a natural human reaction when feeling extreme fear or trauma. She wants to return to a position where she is safe. For me, this gives a feeling of empathy and sadness, because it reminds us of moments when we also just want to hide and not face the noise of life.
